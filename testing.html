DC Supply

The DC supply is built in to the bench where you are working.  It is actually two supplies in one unit, sharing only the ON-OFF switch.  You only need to use one half of it to test your motor.

Each half of the DC supply has two control knobs.  The VOLTAGE control sets the DC voltage that will be maintained between the output terminals.  The range is 0 V to 30 V.  The CURRENT control sets the maximum current that the DC supply will provide.  The range is 0 A to 1 A.  If this current is reached, the supply will automatically reduce the voltage so that this current is not exceeded.

Above the meter, there is a push switch that controls what is shown on the meter.  If the switch is out, the meter shows the voltage between the red and black terminals, on the 0-30 V scale.  If the switch is in, the meter shows the current flowing through the supply, on the 0-1 A scale.

Switch on the DC supply now, before you connect anything to it.  Set the meter to show voltage, and adjust the VOLTAGE control to get 3 V.  This is the voltage that you must use to test your motor.  Set the CURRENT control fully clockwise, for maximum current.

Now switch off the supply while you make the connections.

Connecting Your Motor

Get two connecting wires from the rack on the end of the bench.  Two different colours would be useful - red and black would match the terminals on the DC supply and follow the normal convention.

Connect one end of each wire to a terminal on the DC supply.  Fit a crocodile clip to the other end of each wire.

Now connect the crocodile clips to the stationary parts of the commutator on your motor.  Be careful - the clips will be attracted to the poles of the magnet!

If you need longer wires, you can connect two wires together - there is a socket on the back of each plug.

Testing Your Motor

Switch on the DC supply.  If your motor does not start turning, use something non-metallic to push the coil around.  The coil may be in a position where no current can flow, and you will have to turn it to a position where current can flow before the motor will start.

If your motor does not start, don't panic!  You can do some simple tests to find out what is wrong.

Electrical Tests

First remove the magnet, so that you have better access to the moving parts of your motor.  Set the meter in the DC supply to show current.  Rotate the coil of your motor slowly by hand, while watching the meter.

Is there any position where current flows?  Remember that your design probably expects current to flow for half of each rotation.

If there is no current flowing at any position, you need to find out why not.  Did you remove enough insulation from the copper wire?  Is there good metal-to-metal contact in the commutator?  Are the crocodile clips connected properly, to bare metal?

If there is current flowing at all positions, your motor will not work either.  This could be a problem with the commutator, which is supposed to switch the current on and off as the coil rotates.  There could be a short-circuit between the two places where the crocodile clips are attached...

If the current is flowing for half of each rotation, or a bit less, then you need to check the design of the commutator.  Remember that the current should switch on or off at the positions where the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the magnetic field.

Mechanical Tests

If everything seems OK on the electrical side, replace the magnet and check your motor again.  If it still does not run, you need to look at mechanical problems.

Is there too much friction?  Maybe there is some unintended contact between the rotating parts and some stationary part.

Is the rotating part well balanced?  If you remove the magnet, you should be able to turn the coil to any position, and leave it there.  If the coil always turns to the same position when you release it, it is unbalanced.  It is very difficult to achieve perfect balance, and your motor will run even with a small imbalance.  However, if there is a serious imbalance, it will be very difficult for your motor to run.

If you cannot find the problem, ask your teaching assistant for help. 

Speed Measurement

When your motor is running, fit the disc with the reflective tape onto the axle, if you have not done this already.  The reflective tape should be on the outside, where it is easy to access.  Make sure that the disc is attached securely, so that it turns at the same speed as the motor.  Use a small piece of putty to secure it.

Ask your teaching assistant to measure the speed of your motor.  If this is just a quick measurement, to see how well the motor is running, you will get an estimate of the speed of your motor.  It is only an estimate because the speed is probably varying with time, and the laser tachometer does not take an average.

If you want an official measurement, to be eligible to win the prize for the fastest motor, then we need to measure the average speed by counting the number of revolutions in 15 seconds.

Improvement

When you have your motor running, make sure that you get its speed measured and recorded.  Then you can work on improving your motor, to make it run faster.  You may keep working on this as long as you have time in the laboratory, but at some point you will probably decide that your motor is good enough.

As a guide, a typical speed at the first attempt is about 300 or 400 revolutions per minute (RPM).  The best speeds recorded last year were about 3000 RPM.

The basic requirement for this design exercise is to design and build a working motor, and write a report on your work.  How much time you spend trying to improve it is up to you...
